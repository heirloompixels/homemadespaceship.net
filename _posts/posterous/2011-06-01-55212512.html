We've been wanting for a long time to get some information up on the exact methods we used when building our trailer.  This will take some time and in the end I'll dedicate a few pages to it - but for now I'm going to share the rough drafts of this "how-to" on the blog.  Any feedback would be much appreciated so that I might write these descriptions better. 

So far our techniques are holding up quite well and I have no reservations in recommending them to other people pursuing such a project. 

Much credit is due where it is deserved - most of our methods were learned though many hours of searching the archives of the teardrop forum.  There is a massive wealth of information there along with some really cool trailers.  There is no way we would have finished this project without the knowledge so freely shared in those archives. 

 

Part 1:  Building the walls. 

The goal here is twofold - lightweight and high strength.  I wanted from the get go to keep the trailer as light as possible so that it would be easier to haul around the country.  I looked at a lot of more traditional building methods and really thought about building more along these lines - but the teardrop shape really caught us from the beginning - and to build the teardrop there really was no choice but to go with plywood.  I have a sort of love/hate relationship with this material as it is not "natural" but at the same time it's amazing - truly a wonder and opens up such possibilities. 

The walls are basically "foam-core" - 1/4 inch plywood with 3/4 inch foam insulation sandwitched inbetween.  Two walls are built as "units" and then attached to the trailer as whole pieces.  By laminating the foam to the wood we were able to create a super strong wall - there was very little motion when wiggling it on edge or carrying it around.  At the same time the walls are very lightweight - Jeannie and I were able to carry them with no effort at all.

In order to finish off the edges 1x3 spars are used.  These are run all round the whole whole and in the middle as well to provide places to screw into.  For the curved portions the 1x3's were kerfed - some experimenting and adjustment was needed to figure out how often and how much to kerf. 

The shape was decided upon my trial and error.  We laid out the three pieces of plywood which made up our walls.  As it was the wood we found was 5x4' - which fit perfectly on our 12x5' trailer.  We selected the wood very carefully at the lumber yard - went through the entire stack in fact in order to find grain patters we liked and which fit well with the other pieces.  Back to the shape - we used chalk to draw out the shape - then would step back and look at the shape - erase and readjust, look, readjust, look, readjust... you get the idea.  We spent a bit of time on this as we wanted the arcs to be just so.  Agreed upon Jeannie whom is the power tool master went to work and cut the arcs.  Once the first one was ready the arcs were transfered to the remaining 3 wall sections (2 on each side of the foam) and these were all cut as well - so that we had 4 matching wall pieces.  

All that is left now is to glue everything together.  We did this indoors as it was October and the temperatures were getting below freezing at night - not good for the glue which needs 24 hours above 40 degrees to cure.  

 

There is an obvious order here to the glueing - when working on the sparing you've got to keep cutting to fit the spaces you find.  After the sparing is glued in place you can cut the foam to fit the voids and then glue the whole thing together. 

The sparing on the arcs is glued first. 

Then finish up the sparing for the rest of the walls. 

Fit and glue the foam in place. 

Then add the top walls and weight the whole thing. 

When glueing all this in place you need to use weight to create a good fit.  Unless you own a clamp factory the best option is to find everything available which is heavy - books are great as are plants.  When this doesn't provide enough clamping force you can make nice big weights by filling things with water.  Buckets, storage bins or even a whole bunch of old milk jugs.  Water weighs 8 pounds per gallon so it doesn't take long to get enough heavy objects to provide nice even clamping force on your walls. 

The glue aside: 

Titebond III is the nicest glue to work with - and you can buy big 32 once bottles for around 10 dollars so it is very economical as well. It's waterproof and has a longer open time which gives you a bit of leaway and greatly reduces stress when building.  It also cleans up with a damp rag or water on your hands. Titebond III is also stronger than Gorrilla glue.  (*does not taste good)

Gorrilla Glue can be used when gluing wood to most things wood.  The down side is it foams and makes a mess most of the time and is really had to clean up.  Get it on your hands and you've got to use a solvent to clean it off.  Plus it's a lot more expensive.  

PL 3000 is a specialty glue for use with foam insulation.  We tested both titebond III and Gorrilla glue and they peal right off the foam.  It stinks, comes in chaulk tube form and is expensive - but it does the job at hand. 

And finally you have two walls - just waiting to be assembled into a trailer!
